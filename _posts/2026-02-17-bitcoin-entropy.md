Bitcoin's security depends on pseudorandomness.

Gold can be mined faster if the price rises; fiat can be printed if a crisis hits.

But a 256-bit cryptographic barrier is indifferent to human desire.

### Stored Value, Three Ways

"Gold" is stored energy through the physical difficulty of mining, while "fiat" stores trust in institutional longevity, and "Bitcoin" stores value through entropy and converts it into order.

That is, the creation of wallets, since the private key is a 2^256 random number, and therefore, indifferent to statistical expectations and probabilities averaged over time.

Bitcoin contains true randomness.

### The Vastness of the Key Space

The "randomness" isn't naive; it's so vast that the probability of two people generating the same private key is effectively zero.

For instance, there are estimated to be 10^80 atoms in the observable universe.

Scarcity in the digital world is no longer a physical reality; it is a simulated one.

### The Paradox of Money

The '70s theory on value said that money must be backed by a commodity with intrinsic value, typically gold or silver. While being overcome by any action rooted in government decrees and institutional trust.

Bitcoin represents an attempt to resolve the paradox of money by reducing it to pure numbers; any number is a potential solution.

Algorithmic scarcity enforced through cryptographic randomness demonstrates that numerical precision amplifies rather than eliminates uncertainty, shifting it entirely to price volatility and narrative expectations.

### The Dualism

Hence the paradox: Bitcoin maximizes one side of our dualism. The numerical precision is absolute, the issuance schedule is fixed on a read-and-append-only ledger—exact, immutable. But at the same time, price volatility is extreme, and value narratives swing violently up and down.

The evolution of money is a journey from tangible substance to institutional trust. It traces the shift from the inherent utility of metal to the abstract power of government decree.

Would you trust your money or a value on a random variable?
