"Art is for everybody" is a core idea in Keith Haring's work and life. But at the very core, art is for no one. The contrast with the solitude of the parking, museums, art galleries, and concentration camps is absolute.

### Transaesthetics

The moment consumer culture seeks to sell you a commodity like beauty, it operates through ideological hypocrisy. The artistic object works as a transaesthetic thing, where everything becomes aestheticized, manufactured into a sign for consumption, or a commodity. Art is not the representation of a hidden truth, but becomes part of the obscene unreality of the bourgeois ideology.

### The Brillo Box Paradox

Warhol's soup cans, for instance, are not just a critique, but a reflection of how capitalism aestheticizes everyday lives. When Andy Warhol creates a soap box, the Brillo Box, which could be found in any supermarket, and exhibits it in an art gallery, he does the opposite of celebrating a consumer object, but rather a critique of consumerism itself.

### Art as Rare Flower

Instead of relying on beauty, which is a conformist concept, art should expose the obscene underbelly of the symbolic order. In this sense, true art is uncomfortable, incomprehensible, ephemeral, and unaesthetic, and in the Banksy shredding, something like a rare flower that lives one day.
