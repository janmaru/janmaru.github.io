The **Bongard problem is simple**. The name comes from computer scientist Mikhail Moiseevich Bongard, the first to put in use a meta-rational approach to **problem-solving**.

It's a drawing split into two parts. On both sides of it, usually, six geometrical pictures are drawn. The problem **aims** to find one, and only **one** solution that explains the similarity between the two pairs of six pictures.

The problem could look similar to any IQ logic test provided to students in the past decades. But, it's not.

### What Makes It Different

There are some elements characterizing the Bongard problem.

First, even if there are many parts of a picture, only the **aggregate** of it on one of the two sides counts. The person facing the problem should not **focus** on every single particular element but analyze the overall space given.

They were originally designed as a test domain for artificial intelligence, in the mid-'60s, when AI still looked easy. At the time they had very **limited** progress. The number of properties and relationships that must be recognized and represented is **large**.

### Solving the Problem

**On the left side, all shapes are polygons** (composed entirely of straight line segments). **On the right side, all shapes are curved** (containing smooth, non-straight lines).

**Left Side (Polygons):** The figures include a square, a hexagon, triangles, and a non-convex polygon. All are defined by vertices connected by straight lines.

**Right Side (Curved):** The figures include circles, ovals, and irregular smooth curves (both closed and open). None of these shapes are formed by straight line segments.

### Higher-Order Thinking

Most vision models are trained to identify objects. Bongard problems require identifying relationships and **abstract** properties.

Now AI can switch "levels" of thinking. It's not just looking at the pixels; it's looking for a **higher-order** concept that links the pixels.

AI is mimicking human ability to say, "Forget what the shape is; look at how the line is drawn."

### From Glued to Native

In the "old days" of AI it was usually a specialist. You had one model that could "see," for instance computer vision, and another that could "talk," for instance large language models.

To get them to work together, they had to be **glued** together: the vision model would describe the image in text, and the language model would read that text to give you an answer.

Being "native" means AI has learned to speak both languages at once.

So, now, if you feel at **odds** with life, rethink about it.
