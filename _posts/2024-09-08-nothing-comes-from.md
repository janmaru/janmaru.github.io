**Nothing comes from nothing.**

**Ex nihilo nihil fit.** This idea, first noted by **Parmenides**, suggests that if something can arise from anything, it would violate the law of causality. Every effect must have a cause. Every output must have an input.

But what about randomness? Can a deterministic process produce truly random output?

## The God Conjectures

The **Weak God Conjecture** holds that any deterministic process cannot create genuinely random output. What appears random is merely complex — too complex for us to trace back to its deterministic origin, but deterministic nonetheless.

The **Strong Conjecture** goes further: not only can deterministic processes not produce randomness, but randomness itself may not exist. Everything that appears random is simply a deterministic process we haven't yet understood.

This is why computers use **pseudo-random number generators** (PRNGs). A PRNG takes a seed — a deterministic input — and produces a sequence of numbers that *look* random but are entirely reproducible. Given the same seed, you get the same sequence every time.

Nothing from nothing.

## Conway's Game of Life

In 1970, mathematician **John Conway** introduced the **Game of Life**, a **cellular automaton** that demonstrates how complex behavior can emerge from simple rules.

The game plays out on an infinite two-dimensional grid. Each cell is either alive or dead. At each step, four rules determine the next state:

1. **Underpopulation**: A live cell with fewer than two live neighbors dies.
2. **Survival**: A live cell with two or three live neighbors lives on.
3. **Overpopulation**: A live cell with more than three live neighbors dies.
4. **Reproduction**: A dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes alive.

From these four rules, astonishing complexity emerges — gliders, oscillators, spaceships, even structures that can simulate a Turing machine. All from four deterministic rules and an initial configuration.

## Wolfram's Rule 30

**Stephen Wolfram** took this idea further with one-dimensional cellular automata. **Rule 30** is perhaps the most famous.

The setup is simple: a row of cells, each black or white. At each step, a cell's next state depends on its current state and the states of its two neighbors. That's it — three inputs, one output.

The truth table for Rule 30:

| Current Pattern | 111 | 110 | 101 | 100 | 011 | 010 | 001 | 000 |
|-----------------|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| New State       |  0  |  0  |  0  |  1  |  1  |  1  |  1  |  0  |

Reading the outputs as a binary number: **00011110** = **30** in decimal. Hence the name.

Starting from a single black cell on an otherwise white row, Rule 30 produces a pattern of extraordinary complexity. The left side shows nested, regular structures. The right side appears chaotic, almost random.

## Nature's Rule 30

The pattern generated by Rule 30 bears a striking resemblance to the shell of **Conus textile**, a species of sea snail. The pigmentation patterns on its shell — triangular, chaotic, fractal-like — look as if nature itself is running a cellular automaton.

This is not coincidence. Biological pattern formation often follows rules similar to cellular automata. The pigment cells on the snail's shell deposit color as the shell grows, each cell influenced by its neighbors, producing complex patterns from simple local interactions.

## A TensorFlow Simulation

Running Rule 30 for many iterations and analyzing the output, we find a roughly **uniform distribution of 0s and 1s**. The ratio approaches 50/50 — indistinguishable from a fair coin flip, at least by simple statistical tests.

A deterministic rule, applied to a deterministic initial state, producing output that *appears* random. The Weak God Conjecture in action.

## The Universe as Cellular Automaton

Wolfram took his most radical step in **A New Kind of Science** (2002): the idea that the universe itself might be a massive **cellular automaton**. Physical laws, quantum mechanics, general relativity — all potentially emergent properties of simple, deterministic rules applied at the most fundamental level.

It's a bold conjecture. It remains unproven. But Rule 30 shows that the gap between simplicity and complexity, between determinism and apparent randomness, is far narrower than we might expect.

Nothing comes from nothing. But from almost nothing — a single black cell and a simple rule — comes something that looks very much like everything.
