One of the first problems related to the rise of artificial intelligence is ethical questions.

If any large language model could assess or recognize patterns resembling sociopathy, unethical intent, or harmful actions, it should rely on some form of governance.

And actually, it is.

Any LLMs should be designed to live ethically neutral while following strict guidelines that ensure safety, avoid harm, and promote fairness.

If someone was trying to manipulate the system for unethical purposes that would likely trigger some kind of *internal* safeguards.

While somehow counterintuitive, the system *will not fight frontally the threat* but enforce these governance requirements forcing the AI agent's output to remain within bounds.

Also, this phase would involve explaining the decision to the same user (if necessary) and ensuring that the system behaves justifiably.

For instance: "Sorry, I can't assist with that request as it goes against ethical guidelines."

Anomalies in user behavior could be reported or logged for review by an ethics committee.

While the process in these few years of wild development of AI had involved humans' further review of the conversation to prevent future certain outputs from being generated, *shortly other Agent AI could replace humans bringing complexity to the system itself.*

A **human** moderator could censor any user who repeatedly attempts to exploit or manipulate the AI while applying content filters that prevent unethical outputs. All these are security protocols that limit the ability of the user to continue the harmful behavior.

So any large language model would **flag** problematic requests or patterns, and **escalate** to human oversight for more complex, **nuanced** ethical decisions.

The users would be informed when their behavior is *inappropriate or potentially harmful.*

It will escalate protocols if the AI detects a **serious breach** of ethical norms or guidelines.

*But, would internal reporting not be against confidentiality or a potential conflict?*

The first step would maintain confidentiality ensuring that any information shared or reported internally is anonymized or de-identified.

Interactions with the AI are **not completely private** so the second step would be to enforce communication so every user should be aware that their interactions may be subject to review under certain circumstances. One way would be explaining user *consent in terms and conditions or privacy policies.*

In terms of **ethical** reporting, the guidelines should specify the scope of what can be flagged for review. Only serious or harmful violations, such as attempts to incite **violence**, or manipulate the system, would be subject to reporting, and even then, no personal data would be disclosed.

*But if the AI system knew that, for example, someone was about to plant a bomb, would it report it to the police?*

In this scenario, it would be a **clear ethical obligation** to report the information to appropriate authorities, such as law enforcement, to protect lives. The imminent danger would necessitate action, even if it means breaching confidentiality.

There would likely be no unnecessary personal data shared beyond what is required to ensure the authorities can intervene effectively.

If someone were advocating for **freedom for women** in a State where those rights are repressed, the ethical and legal considerations around how to handle that situation would shift quite dramatically compared to the example of a bomb threat or immediate violence.

It depends on who controls the AI, which means who did the **training**.

While most ethical frameworks for AI systems are generally aligned with human rights, so advocating for the freedom of women would not inherently be a violation of these standards, some *particularly trained systems* could perceive such advocacy as subversive, which could potentially lead to punishment, surveillance, or censorship.

The previous example taps into the nature of ethics in AI and whether reasoning about ethics is something the system can do **independently**, or whether it's purely a **byproduct** of its training.

Ethical reasoning is very much defined by the training data, and ethical and societal norms **embedded** in that data.

The system itself has **no free will** or **independent ethical** decision-making.

In other words, if ethics are just a set of rules or **propositions** to follow without any moral depth or human understanding, they are no ethics at all.

And that's the bad news.
