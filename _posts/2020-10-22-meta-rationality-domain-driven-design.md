The **Bongard problem** is simple.

The problem aims to find one, and only one solution that explains the similarity between two pairs of six pictures. On the left side, six images share a common property. On the right side, six images share the opposite property. The task is to identify the rule.

What makes it interesting is that only the **aggregate** matters, not the individual elements. A single image on its own tells you nothing. It's the relationship between the groups — the pattern that emerges from comparison — that reveals the answer.

This is a **meta-rational** approach. You don't solve the problem by analyzing each picture in isolation. You solve it by stepping back and seeing the whole.

## Domain-Driven Design

**Domain-Driven Design** (DDD), introduced by Eric Evans, is built on similar principles:

- **Domain**: the subject area the software addresses
- **Model**: an abstraction of the domain that captures its essential structure
- **Ubiquitous Language**: a shared vocabulary between developers and domain experts
- **Bounded Contexts**: explicit boundaries within which a model applies

The model is not the domain. The map is not the territory. But a good model captures enough of the territory to be useful.

And the **Ubiquitous Language** is where things get complicated. Because language is ambiguous.

## The Ambiguity of Language

**Ferdinand de Saussure** taught us that the relationship between the **signifier** (the word, the sound) and the **signified** (the concept, the meaning) is **arbitrary**. There is no inherent reason why the word "tree" refers to a tree. It's a social convention.

**Émile Benveniste** pushed back: the connection between signifier and signified is **necessary**, but only within a cultural context. For a French speaker, "arbre" *is* a tree. The arbitrariness is invisible from the inside.

**Charles Sanders Peirce** offered a different framework entirely: the **Triadic Model**. A sign has three components — the **sign** itself, the **object** it refers to, and the **interpretant** (the meaning created in the mind of the interpreter). Meaning is not fixed; it's generated through interpretation.

And then there is **Umberto Eco**.

## Eco and the Infinity of Interpretation

Eco argued that a text can be **"used"** or **"interpreted."** To use a text is to extract from it whatever serves your purpose, regardless of the author's intent. To interpret a text is to engage with it on its own terms, within the limits set by the text itself.

But even interpretation is not fixed. Any text generates an **infinity of interpretations**. The text constrains, but it does not determine.

Consider the motto of the Three Musketeers:

> **"Tous pour un, un pour tous!"**

To a French reader in the 19th century, this evokes camaraderie, honor, the spirit of the ancien régime. To a modern reader, it might evoke teamwork, solidarity, or even cliché. The words are the same. The meaning shifts with cultural context.

## Meta-Rationality

**Meta-rationality** recognizes that reality is both **intelligible** and **chaotic**. Systems of meaning — whether in language, in software design, or in science — are useful approximations, not perfect representations.

A total systematic approach is doomed to failure. No formal system can capture all the nuances of a complex domain. There will always be edge cases, ambiguities, exceptions that fall outside the model.

DDD acknowledges this. The Bounded Context is an admission that no single model can describe everything. The Ubiquitous Language is an attempt to reduce ambiguity, not eliminate it.

## The Model as Convergence

If the domain is described meta-rationally — through a series of texts, conversations, diagrams, examples — we might represent this as:

**(A, B, C, D, E) => M**

Where **A, B, C, D, E** are different descriptions, perspectives, and artifacts, and **M** is the model that emerges from their convergence.

The model is not derived from any single source. It's the aggregate that matters — just like in the Bongard problem.

No individual description is the truth. But together, they point toward something useful.
