Knowing a **little** of something is worse than knowing **nothing** at all.

An underfitted AI model sees a complex, nonlinear world as a straight line. It has **captured** something but misses the entire intricate spider web of connections.

### Why Is Something Worse Than None?

If a model knows nothing—for instance random noise—nobody will **trust** it. But an underfitted model provides consistently wrong answers with high statistical confidence. What is a false map to heaven, that, instead, leads you to confident, incorrect **hellish** predictions?

In the age of agentic systems, AI that doesn't just predict text, but uses tools and writes code to achieve goals. Here, knowing a little moves from a statistical error to a **functional** catastrophe.

### The Illusion of Competence

Is the **illusion** of competence—when an AI model knows nothing and produces apparent chaos, and humans are naturally wired to reject chaos—different from a **coherent** lie?

If the model has already "decided" what the world looks like before seeing the data, and the AI agent moves from generating text to executing code, knowing a little becomes a force—but a force that multiplies errors.

### The Chain of Flawed Thoughts

The **chain** of thoughts has a logical flaw in reasoning. If the underlying model is underfitted, it creates a false **reality** based on flawed premises. An agent tasked with reducing energy cost is bound to choose **turning off hardware** since it reduces costs.

People with AI in front of their names are bound to turn off their brains.
