It is always difficult to know where to **begin**.

For Kierkegaard, a beginning is not about time but an existential "leap" that cannot be inferred from its premises. In the Bible, Abraham's decision to sacrifice Isaac is not the outcome of an ethical syllogism but a break, a teleological suspension of the ethical, that creates an entirely new relation to God.

The ethical command "Thou shalt not kill" is for a higher purpose; a true **inquiry** often requires us to suspend our "common sense" or "logical" expectations to see what lies beneath.

### Beyond the Universal

This beginning cannot be fully translated back into the language of the universal; it exceeds ethical and speculative categories and therefore resists normal narrative closure or rational reconstruction.

### Repetition as a New Beginning

In *Repetition* (*Gjentagelse*), the category of repetition is precisely Kierkegaard's way of thinking the paradox of a beginning that is "again." Genuine repetition is not mere return of the same nor Greek recollection but a forward movement in which what once was becomes truly new.

The beginning is thus not **immutable**; it is an event that must be continuously re-begun in time, and this "again" has a religious structure, rather than a purely metaphysical one.

It is often best to let any journey **unfold** itself.
