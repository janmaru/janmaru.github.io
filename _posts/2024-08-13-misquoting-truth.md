When **George E. P. Box** wrote that **"all models are wrong, but some are useful,"** he wanted to point out that even if all models somehow make sense, it's pivotal how they can explain, predict, or manage data.

A model is a simplification. It leaves things out. That's the point.

**Peter Norvig** took this further: **"All models are wrong, and increasingly you can succeed without them."** The implication is clear — with enough data, perhaps the model becomes unnecessary.

**Chris Anderson**, in his 2008 *Wired* article **"The End of Theory,"** made the boldest claim of all: correlation is enough. Numbers speak for themselves. We don't need hypotheses. We don't need theories. We just need data.

But do numbers really speak for themselves?

## Aliquid Stat Pro Aliquo

The question of how signs carry meaning is ancient. **Saint Augustine** formulated the principle: **"aliquid stat pro aliquo"** — something stands for something else.

A word stands for a thing. A symbol stands for a concept. A data point stands for a measurement.

But *how* does the standing work? What connects the sign to what it signifies?

## Saussure: The Arbitrary Sign

**Ferdinand de Saussure** answered: the relationship between **signifier** (the word, the sound, the mark) and **signified** (the concept, the meaning) is **arbitrary**. There is no natural bond between the sound "dog" and the animal it refers to.

The **Rosetta Stone** makes this vivid. The same decree appears in three scripts — hieroglyphic, demotic, and Greek. When the social context that connected those signs to their meanings was lost, the arbitrariness became evident. It took Champollion decades to reconstruct the connection.

## Peirce: The Triadic Model

**Charles Sanders Peirce** proposed a richer framework. A sign has three components:

- The **sign** (or representamen) — the form the sign takes
- The **object** — what the sign refers to
- The **interpretant** — the meaning generated in the interpreter's mind

Meaning is not a fixed property of the sign. It emerges through the act of interpretation, shaped by context, culture, and prior knowledge.

Consider **Saint Augustine's cross**. To a Christian, it signifies salvation, sacrifice, the central narrative of their faith. To an indigenous people encountering European colonizers for the first time, it might signify power, domination, or simply a strange geometric shape. The sign is the same. The interpretant is radically different.

## Barthes: Mythology

**Roland Barthes** in **Mythologies** showed how cultural objects function as a social language. A magazine cover, a wrestling match, a plate of steak and frites — each carries layers of meaning beyond its literal content. These are **myths**: second-order sign systems built on top of first-order denotation.

**Andy Warhol's Brillo Box** operates in this space. It looks like a Brillo box. But placed in a gallery, it becomes a criticism of consumerism, a question about what distinguishes art from commodity, an act of institutional critique.

## Derrida: The Image and the Imaginer

**Jacques Derrida** argued that the meaning of images is created by the person who imagines them. There is no transcendental signified — no ultimate, fixed meaning anchoring the chain of signs. Meaning is always deferred, always relational, always dependent on context.

## Back to Data

We now have two extremes:

1. **Data is more convincing than models.** Numbers speak for themselves. Correlation is enough.
2. **Data makes no sense on its own.** Without a framework of interpretation, data points are just noise.

The history of semiotics tells us that the second position is closer to the truth. Signs don't carry meaning inherently. Meaning is constructed through interpretation, context, and cultural codes.

## Eco as Pragmatist

**Umberto Eco** offers a middle path. The question is not **"What do I see?"** but **"How is meaning generated through understanding?"**

Eco recognized that interpretation is constrained but not determined. A text (or a dataset) limits the range of valid interpretations, but it does not dictate a single one. The interpreter brings their own codes, their own context, their own history.

## Machine Learning and Interpretation

Machine learning systems interpret data based on **codes learned during training**. These codes are influenced by the architecture of the model, the quality and distribution of the training data, and the context in which the system operates.

A model trained on biased data will produce biased interpretations. A model trained on narrow data will fail on out-of-distribution inputs. The data does not speak for itself — the model speaks *through* the data, shaped by its training and its structure.

Box was right: all models are wrong. But without models, data is mute. The art is in choosing the right model for the right purpose — knowing its limitations, understanding its assumptions, and never mistaking its output for truth.
